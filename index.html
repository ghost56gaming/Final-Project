Our solar system is made up of eight incredible planets, each with unique characteristics, atmospheres, and histories. From the scorching surface of Mercury to the icy winds of Neptune, these worlds offer endless opportunities for exploration and wonder.

The smallest planet and closest to the Sun, Mercury has extreme temperatures and no atmosphere to retain heat.

Known as Earth’s twin in size, Venus is covered in thick clouds of sulfuric acid and has surface temperatures hot enough to melt lead.

Our home planet and the only known world with life, Earth has vast oceans, diverse climates, and a protective atmosphere.

Often called the Red Planet, Mars has the largest volcano in the solar system and may once have had liquid water on its surface.

The largest planet, Jupiter is a gas giant with powerful storms, including the Great Red Spot, and more than 90 moons.

Famous for its stunning rings made of ice and rock, Saturn is a gas giant with dozens of fascinating moons.

An icy giant tipped on its side, Uranus has a bluish color due to methane gas in its atmosphere and rotates almost horizontally.

The farthest planet, Neptune is a deep blue ice giant with supersonic winds and violent storms.

Planets are important because they are essential for supporting life, providing necessary resources like air, water, and food, and serving as our home and shelter. They also play a crucial role in maintaining the stability of our solar system, influencing gravity, and contributing to the evolution of life. By understanding planets, we can better manage our relationship with our planet and ensure its long-term health and prosperity.

Planets are round because their own gravity pulls their material inward equally from all directions, a force strong enough to overcome the material's strength and form a sphere. This inward pull shapes newly forming planets into spheres, while smaller objects like asteroids and comets have weaker gravity and remain irregularly shaped. A planet's rotation also causes it to bulge slightly at the equator, making it an oblate spheroid rather than a perfect sphere.
